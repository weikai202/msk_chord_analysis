Racial and Ethnic Disparities in Driver Gene Mutations in Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer

Leveraging MSK-CHORD 2024 (n = 25,040) to examine whether driver gene mutation frequencies differ by race/ethnicity and how those differences associate with overall survival.

7,775 NSCLC patients
8 Driver genes
5 Race/ethnic groups
3 Cancer cohorts
↓ View Figures ⭐ GitHub Repository

Project Overview

A five-step reproducible analysis pipeline examining the intersection of race/ethnicity, tumour genomics, and survival outcomes in lung cancer.

📊

Descriptive Analysis

Table 1 and Wilson-CI prevalence bar charts stratified by race/ethnicity across all driver genes.

🧮

Association Tests

Chi-square / Fisher's exact tests with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction and Cramér's V effect sizes.

🌲

Logistic Regression

Multivariable adjusted odds ratios controlling for smoking, stage, histology, sex, and age.

📈

Survival Analysis

Kaplan-Meier curves, log-rank tests, and Cox PH model with a Race × EGFR interaction term.

🔁

Replication

Independent validation of key findings in colorectal and breast cancer cohorts from the same dataset.

🛡️

Sensitivity Analysis

MICE multiple imputation (m = 20) compared with complete-case results via Rubin's rules.

Key Findings

Three robust findings replicated after multivariable adjustment.

EGFR enrichment in Asian patients (adj. OR 2.93, 95% CI 2.43–3.53, FDR < 0.001) Asian patients carry a 39.1% EGFR mutation rate vs. 20.2% in White patients. This association survives full adjustment for smoking, stage, histology, sex, and age, and is specific to NSCLC — not observed in CRC or Breast cohorts.
KRAS / STK11 deficit in Asian patients (KRAS adj. OR 0.47; STK11 adj. OR 0.38) White patients have the highest smoking rate (78.8%) and correspondingly the highest KRAS (31.2%) and STK11 (14.5%) mutation rates. After adjustment these deficits in Asian patients remain significant, suggesting both smoking-mediated and biology-mediated components.
Partial survival mediation through EGFR biology Asian patients with EGFR-mutant tumours have median OS of 49.1 months vs. 48.2 months in White EGFR-mutant patients. The Cox Race × EGFR interaction term is not significant after full adjustment, suggesting the survival advantage operates through treatment access rather than differential mutation biology.
Black patients: youngest median age (67 yr), highest Stage IV rate (50.8%), shortest OS (18.7 mo) After adjusting for stage and smoking, Black patients' survival disadvantage attenuates but does not disappear — consistent with residual confounding by treatment access or unmeasured socioeconomic factors.

FIG 4 Median overall survival by race/ethnicity and EGFR mutation status

Each point represents the Kaplan-Meier median OS for a race × EGFR-status subgroup. Triangles denote EGFR-mutant patients; circles denote EGFR wild-type patients. EGFR mutation consistently improves median OS within every racial group. The EGFR-mutant survival advantage is largest for Asian and Hispanic patients (~20 months improvement), compared with ~11 months for White patients.

FIG 5 Replication across NSCLC, CRC, and Breast cohorts

Cross-cohort replication of adjusted ORs for EGFR, KRAS, and TP53 in Asian and Black patients. EGFR enrichment in Asian patients is specific to NSCLC (OR 2.93) and attenuates toward null in CRC and Breast — supporting a lung-tissue-specific biological mechanism rather than a pan-cancer racial genomic pattern. TP53 enrichment in Black patients is significant in Breast (OR 1.93) and CRC, suggesting a cancer-type-specific effect.

Statistical Methods

A five-step pipeline from data cleaning through survival analysis, implemented in R with full reproducibility.

Step 1 Data Processing

Downloaded from cBioPortal via cbioportalR. Race/ethnicity harmonised to OMB classification. Critical fix: "Non-Spanish; Non-Hispanic" checked before "Hispanic" in case_when to prevent ~5,000 misclassifications. MICE imputation (m = 20, maxit = 10) for sensitivity analysis.

Step 2 Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 generated with gtsummary. Mutation prevalence with Wilson confidence intervals, preferred over Wald CIs for proportions near 0 or 1 (Wilson 1927). Group differences assessed by Kruskal-Wallis (continuous) and chi-square (categorical).

Step 3 Association Tests

Chi-square used when all expected cell counts ≥ 5; Fisher's exact test (B = 10,000 simulations) otherwise. Multiple testing corrected by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at q = 0.05. Effect size measured by Cramér's V.

Step 4 Logistic Regression

Separate binary outcome (MUT_gene ~ RACE_ETH + SMOKING + STAGE + SEX + AGE + HIST3) for each of 8 genes. HISTOLOGY collapsed to 3 categories to avoid complete separation. Falls back to Firth penalised regression if standard GLM fails. Events-per-variable (EPV) checked before fitting.

Step 5 Survival Analysis

Kaplan-Meier + log-rank test. Multivariable Cox PH with RACE_ETH × MUT_EGFR interaction term. Proportional hazards assumption tested via Schoenfeld residuals (cox.zph()). Tied event times handled by Efron's method.

Step 6 Replication

Same logistic regression pipeline applied to independent CRC and Breast cohorts from MSK-CHORD 2024. Smoking history included only when > 50% complete. Three-cohort forest plot visualises consistency vs. tissue-specificity.
